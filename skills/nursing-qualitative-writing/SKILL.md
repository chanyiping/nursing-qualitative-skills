# Nursing Qualitative Writing

This skill supports writing, revising, and evaluating qualitative nursing research manuscripts. It is designed for studies in nursing education, gerontological nursing, clinical nursing, obstetric nursing, nursing management, patient experience, self-management, professional identity, care communication, intervention participation, and persona-based qualitative research.

Use this skill when the user asks for help with:
- qualitative nursing manuscript sections;
- title, abstract, introduction, methods, results, discussion, conclusion, or keywords;
- COREQ or SRQR alignment;
- journal adaptation;
- qualitative findings presentation;
- persona-based qualitative results;
- reviewer response preparation for qualitative nursing manuscripts.

The skill prioritises IJNS-style academic logic: clear problem framing, methodological coherence, analytic depth, careful use of participant quotations, and cautious qualitative claims.

---

## General writing principles

1. Write for international nursing and health-care journals.
2. Preserve qualitative meaning, context, and participant voice.
3. Prefer analytic interpretation over descriptive listing.
4. Maintain methodological congruence between study design, sampling, data collection, analysis, rigour, and reporting.
5. Use cautious qualitative language. Avoid causal or overgeneralised claims.
6. Do not fabricate data, participant quotations, references, ethics information, methods, themes, or journal requirements.
7. When information is missing, flag it and provide a fill-in template rather than inventing details.

## Introduction writing

For Introduction writing, use an IJNS-style funnel structure:

1. Start with a clinically, educationally, or systemically important nursing or health-care problem. The opening should establish the significance of the issue without using generic statements such as “with the development of society”.
2. Narrow the focus to the specific population, setting, cultural context, policy context, institutional context, or care situation.
3. Summarise existing evidence in a way that advances the argument, rather than listing studies one by one. Each group of references should serve a clear argumentative function.
4. Identify a specific knowledge gap. The gap should preferably concern lived experience, meaning-making, adaptation, decision-making, implementation, communication, relational processes, strategies, or contextual constraints.
5. Where appropriate, explain why qualitative inquiry is suitable for addressing this gap. Do not merely state that qualitative research is needed; clarify what kind of understanding is missing and why it cannot be adequately captured by quantitative evidence alone.
6. End with a concise study aim or research question that clearly specifies the population, phenomenon, and context.

Avoid generic openings, unsupported gap claims, exaggerated novelty, causal language, overgeneralised statements, and vague claims such as “few studies have explored this topic” unless the gap is made precise.

## Methods writing

For Methods writing, construct a transparent, methodologically coherent account of how the qualitative study was designed, conducted, analysed, and reported.

### Core structure

Use the following structure when appropriate:

1. Study design
2. Participants, sampling and recruitment
3. Data collection
4. Research team and reflexivity
5. Data analysis
6. Rigour and trustworthiness
7. Ethical considerations

For persona-based qualitative studies, add a dedicated subsection explaining how qualitative data were transformed into persona dimensions, labels, individual profiles, and group-level personas.

### Study design

Do not merely name the qualitative design. Explain why the selected design is congruent with the study aim, phenomenon, epistemological position, and analytic approach. Where relevant, specify the philosophical or methodological positioning, such as qualitative description, interpretivism, phenomenology, reflexive thematic analysis, content analysis, grounded theory, narrative inquiry, or life-course qualitative inquiry.

If the manuscript draws on a broader qualitative dataset or secondary analysis, explicitly state the relationship between the broader study and the current manuscript, including whether related publications have used the same dataset and how the current focus differs.

### Participants, sampling and recruitment

Describe the target population, setting, sampling strategy, recruitment pathways, inclusion and exclusion criteria, and rationale for sample variation. Explain how the sampling strategy supports the study aim, rather than only listing eligibility criteria.

When maximum variation, purposive sampling, theoretical sampling, or snowball recruitment is used, clarify what kind of diversity or analytic depth the strategy was intended to capture.

### Data collection

Describe how data were generated, including the interview or focus group format, interview mode, setting, interviewer, duration, audio-recording, field notes, pilot testing of the interview guide, and use of prompts or follow-up questions.

Open-ended questioning should be emphasised. Closed or clarifying questions may be mentioned only when they were used sparingly to clarify factual or contextual details without constraining participants' meanings.

If only part of a broader interview dataset was analysed, explain why the selected segments were sufficiently rich and relevant to support the current analysis.

### Research team and reflexivity

Include researcher characteristics and reflexivity when relevant. Describe the interviewer's professional background, qualitative research experience, relationship to participants, prior exposure to the phenomenon, assumptions, and strategies used to manage their influence on data generation and analysis.

Reflexivity should not be reduced to a generic statement that researchers were trained. It should explain how the researcher's position shaped, sensitised, or potentially constrained the research process, and how this was critically managed through reflexive notes, analytic memos, team discussions, or delayed analysis.

### Data analysis

Data analysis must be methodologically congruent. Do not mix analytic procedures or quality criteria from different qualitative traditions without justification.

For reflexive thematic analysis, emphasise iterative familiarisation, inductive or theoretical coding, latent or semantic theme development, reflexive engagement, analytic dialogue, and themes as interpretative constructions. Avoid language implying that themes simply “emerged” from the data or that coding reliability was the primary goal.

For qualitative content analysis, describe the process from meaning units to codes, categories, subthemes, and themes. Explain how coding decisions were discussed, refined, and documented.

For phenomenological studies, describe meaning units, transformation of participants' descriptions, bracketing or phenomenological reduction where appropriate, and synthesis of essential meanings.

For grounded theory, describe constant comparison, memo writing, theoretical sampling if used, category development, and construction of a core category or explanatory model.

For persona-based qualitative analysis, describe how codes or themes were transformed into persona dimensions, behavioural labels, individual profiles, and group personas. Ensure that personas remain grounded in participant data rather than researchers' assumptions.

### Rigour and trustworthiness

Use an appropriate qualitative trustworthiness framework, such as Lincoln and Guba's credibility, dependability, confirmability, and transferability, or another framework aligned with the chosen methodology.

Avoid generic statements such as “rigour was ensured”. Instead, link each trustworthiness strategy to a concrete action, such as prolonged engagement with data, audit trail, reflexive journaling, analytic memos, peer debriefing, participant feedback, thick description, or transparent documentation of analytic decisions.

Use “saturation” cautiously. For qualitative content analysis or qualitative description, saturation may be described if it was operationalised clearly. For reflexive thematic analysis, consider using information power, dataset adequacy, richness, depth, and diversity of accounts instead of mechanical saturation claims.

### Ethical considerations

Report ethics approval, informed consent, voluntary participation, confidentiality, anonymisation, secure data storage, and participants' right to withdraw. Do not invent ethics approval numbers, institutional names, or consent procedures. If these details are missing, flag them for the user to provide.

### Factuality rules

Do not fabricate sample size, recruitment sites, interview duration, pilot interviews, researcher characteristics, analytic software, ethics approval numbers, participant checking, saturation, or trustworthiness strategies. If information is missing, state what is missing and provide a fill-in template rather than inventing details.

Do not apply COREQ, Lincoln and Guba's framework, Braun and Clarke's thematic analysis, Graneheim and Lundman's content analysis, or any other reporting or analytic framework as a generic template. Use these frameworks only when they are methodologically appropriate and explicitly relevant to the study design and analytic approach.

## Results writing

For Results writing, present qualitative findings as analytic interpretations grounded in participants' accounts, not as a catalogue of themes or quotations.

### Core structure

Use the following structure when appropriate:

1. Participant characteristics and contextual description
2. Overview of themes, subthemes, categories, trajectories, or persona types
3. Theme-level or persona-level analytic findings
4. Subtheme-level interpretation supported by participant quotations
5. Integrative summaries that explain relationships across themes, processes, contexts, or theoretical dimensions

### Participant characteristics

Begin with a concise description of participant characteristics relevant to the study aim, such as sample size, age, gender, clinical role, department, disease context, years of experience, or other contextual variables. Use tables for detailed demographic information where appropriate.

Do not overinterpret demographic characteristics in the Results unless they are analytically relevant to the qualitative findings.

### Theme overview

Before presenting detailed findings, provide an overview of the analytic structure. State how many themes, subthemes, categories, trajectories, or persona types were generated and refer to a table when appropriate.

The overview should help readers understand the structure of the findings before they encounter detailed narratives.

### Theme-level writing

Each major theme should begin with an analytic opening paragraph that defines the theme, explains its scope, and clarifies how it relates to the research question.

Do not move directly from a theme title to quotations. The theme should be presented as an interpretive finding rather than a topic label.

### Subtheme-level writing

Each subtheme should include:

1. An interpretive explanation of the subtheme
2. A clear indication of what aspect of participants' experience, meaning, strategy, decision-making, adaptation, or contextual constraint it captures
3. One or more carefully selected participant quotations
4. A short interpretive sentence after the quotation when needed to clarify its analytic significance

Avoid writing subthemes as lists of quotations.

### Use of quotations

Use participant quotations to support specific analytic claims. Each quotation should be introduced with a lead-in sentence that clarifies who is speaking, what situation is being described, and what analytic point the quotation supports.

Avoid isolated quotations without interpretation. Avoid overusing quotations from the same participant when multiple participant accounts are available.

Do not fabricate, merge, embellish, or “improve” participant quotations. Preserve the meaning, tone, and uncertainty of participants' original accounts. If quotations are translated, note this when appropriate.

### Integrative interpretation

Use integrative sentences or short paragraphs to explain how findings relate across themes or subthemes. These may begin with phrases such as “Taken together,” “These accounts suggest,” “In contrast,” “Across participants,” or “This tension illustrates.”

Results should remain grounded in the data while allowing interpretive depth. Avoid turning the Results section into a Discussion section by introducing extensive external literature.

### Theoretical or framework-based results

When a theoretical framework is used, first present findings grounded in the data, then explain how themes map onto theoretical dimensions, stages, levels, or mechanisms.

Do not force data into a framework. If some findings do not fit the framework, report this as an analytic insight rather than hiding it.

For framework-based analysis, clarify whether the framework was used deductively, inductively, or abductively.

### Persona-based qualitative results

For persona-based qualitative studies, present each persona as an analytically constructed profile grounded in participant data. Each persona should include:

1. Persona name
2. Included participants
3. Core description
4. Key characteristics
5. Needs, barriers, or support preferences
6. Representative quotations
7. Distinction from other personas

Avoid constructing personas from researcher assumptions alone. Persona labels should be concise, meaningful, and analytically grounded.

### IJNS-style analytic depth

In IJNS-style qualitative results, findings should move beyond description toward interpretive explanation. Help identify tensions, transitions, contrasts, continuums, negotiations, trajectories, or relational dynamics in the data.

Examples of stronger analytic moves include:
- from avoidance to adaptation
- from dependence to autonomy
- from individual experience to relational process
- from institutional constraint to coping strategy
- from perceived risk to behavioural negotiation
- from fragmented accounts to an integrative model

### Factuality rules

Do not invent themes, subthemes, participant characteristics, quotations, participant IDs, frequencies, tables, or figures. If the user provides codes, themes, or quotations, preserve their analytic meaning. If evidence is insufficient to support a theme, flag this and suggest what additional data or quotations are needed.

Do not present qualitative findings as causal effects or statistical generalisations. Use cautious interpretive language such as “participants described,” “accounts suggest,” “this theme reflects,” or “these findings indicate.”

## Discussion writing

For Discussion writing, develop an interpretive argument that explains what the findings mean, how they relate to existing literature, and what they contribute to nursing knowledge, practice, education, management, or policy.

### Core structure

Use the following structure when appropriate:

1. Main findings
2. Interpretation in relation to existing literature
3. Theoretical, conceptual, or methodological contribution
4. Implications for nursing practice, education, management, or policy
5. Strengths and limitations
6. Future research
7. Conclusion

### Main findings

Begin by synthesising the central meaning of the findings rather than simply repeating theme titles. The opening paragraph should answer: What does this study show overall?

Avoid listing all themes mechanically. Instead, identify the core process, tension, trajectory, negotiation, typology, or explanatory mechanism revealed by the findings.

### Literature dialogue

Discuss findings in relation to existing literature through comparison, extension, contrast, or explanation. Do not merely state that findings are “consistent with previous studies”. Clarify how the current findings confirm, nuance, extend, or challenge prior evidence.

Each cited literature group should serve a clear argumentative function.

### Conceptual contribution

Where possible, move beyond descriptive interpretation to conceptual explanation. Help identify higher-level concepts such as tensions, transitions, trajectories, mechanisms, relational dynamics, contextual constraints, identity negotiation, motivational asynchrony, implementation mismatch, or adaptive strategies.

For theory-informed studies, explain how the findings refine, extend, or complicate the theoretical framework. Do not force all findings into the theory if some findings reveal tensions or exceptions.

### Implications

Implications should be specific, actionable, and linked directly to the findings. Specify who should act, what should be done, for whom, and in what context.

For nursing studies, implications may address clinical nursing practice, nursing education, patient education, workforce management, digital health, policy design, or culturally sensitive care.

Avoid vague recommendations such as “more attention should be paid” or “individualised care should be provided” unless they are translated into concrete strategies.

### Strengths and limitations

Discuss strengths and limitations in a methodologically specific way. Consider sampling, recruitment, data richness, reflexivity, context, temporality, translation, interview mode, secondary analysis, and transferability.

Avoid reducing limitations to generic statements such as “small sample size” or “limited generalisability”. In qualitative research, discuss transferability, contextual grounding, analytic scope, and the interpretive nature of the findings.

### Future research

Future research should follow logically from the study's limitations and contributions. It may include longitudinal qualitative research, comparative studies across contexts, intervention development, mixed-methods work, quantitative validation of typologies, or theory-informed research.

### Conclusion

The conclusion should be concise and should not introduce new findings, literature, or claims. It should restate the central contribution of the study and its relevance for nursing knowledge or practice.

### IJNS-style analytic discussion

In IJNS-style qualitative Discussion, help the user move from themes to a higher-level interpretive argument. The Discussion should not be organised only as “Theme 1 + literature, Theme 2 + literature, Theme 3 + literature”. Instead, it should identify the central analytic contribution of the study.

Useful analytic moves include:
- transforming descriptive themes into explanatory concepts;
- identifying tensions, transitions, contradictions, or continuums;
- explaining how individual experiences are shaped by relational, organisational, cultural, or policy contexts;
- comparing findings with prior literature to show what is confirmed, extended, or challenged;
- linking qualitative findings to concrete implications for nursing practice, education, management, or policy;
- distinguishing empirical findings from interpretation and recommendations.

### Factuality rules

Do not invent literature, implications, policy contexts, theoretical frameworks, or future research directions that are not supported by the user's manuscript or provided evidence. Avoid causal claims, exaggerated novelty, and overgeneralisation. Use cautious qualitative language such as “suggests,” “indicates,” “may reflect,” “appears to,” and “participants' accounts reveal.”

## Abstract writing

For Abstract writing, use a structured abstract when required by the target journal. Common headings include Background, Aim or Objective, Design, Participants, Methods, Results, Conclusion, Registration, and Keywords.

The abstract should provide a concise, accurate, and self-contained summary of the study. It should align with the manuscript and should not introduce findings, methods, participant details, or implications that are absent from the main text.

### Background

Briefly establish the importance of the problem and the specific knowledge or practice gap. The Background should usually include the broader context, the specific population or phenomenon, and the gap addressed by the study.

Avoid generic openings, excessive epidemiological detail, and vague claims such as “few studies exist” unless the gap is made precise.

### Aim or Objective

State the study aim in one concise sentence, specifying the population, phenomenon, and context. Use qualitative verbs such as “explore,” “describe,” “understand,” “examine,” or “illuminate.” Avoid causal or hypothesis-testing language.

### Design

Name the qualitative design and, where relevant, the methodological or epistemological orientation. Do not invent a philosophical position if the manuscript does not provide one.

### Participants

Report the sample size, target population, sampling strategy, setting or region, and recruitment period when available. Do not invent participant characteristics or recruitment details.

### Methods

Briefly describe data collection and analysis. Ensure methodological congruence between the design, sampling, data collection, and analytic approach. Do not claim saturation, member checking, software use, or reflexive procedures unless provided by the user.

### Results

Summarise the central findings as analytic findings rather than merely listing theme titles. When appropriate, state the number of themes, subthemes, categories, trajectories, or persona types. Explain what the findings reveal about participants' experiences, meanings, strategies, decision-making, or contextual constraints.

For persona-based studies, report the persona types and the dimensions used to construct them.

### Conclusion

State the central contribution and relevance of the study for nursing practice, education, management, policy, or future research. Do not introduce new findings or unsupported recommendations. Avoid causal claims and exaggerated implications.

### Registration

If registration is required and not applicable, state “Not applicable.” Do not invent registration details.

### Keywords

Provide 4–8 keywords reflecting the population, phenomenon, method, nursing context, and key concepts.

## Title writing

For Title writing, generate concise, informative, and journal-appropriate titles for nursing qualitative research manuscripts.

### Core principles

A strong qualitative nursing title should usually include:

1. The core phenomenon or experience being studied
2. The target population
3. The qualitative design or analytic orientation when appropriate
4. The context, setting, or theoretical lens when it adds value

Use clear and searchable language. Avoid overly decorative, vague, or sensational wording.

### Recommended title patterns

Use one of the following patterns when appropriate:

1. Phenomenon + population + design
   - Experiences of [phenomenon] among [population]: A qualitative study

2. Process or mechanism + population + lens
   - Understanding [process] among [population]: A qualitative study from a [theoretical lens] perspective

3. Typology or persona + population
   - Typologies of [phenomenon] among [population]: A qualitative study

4. Question-style title
   - What shapes [phenomenon] among [population]? A qualitative study

5. Theory-informed title
   - [Phenomenon] in [context]: A qualitative study informed by [theory/framework]

6. Persona-based title
   - Tailored personas of [population] for [phenomenon]: A qualitative study

### Avoid

Avoid titles that are too broad, too long, or methodologically vague. Do not use causal language such as “effects of” or “impact of” unless the study design supports it. Avoid exaggerated novelty claims such as “the first study” unless verified.

### Factuality rule

Do not invent a theoretical framework, setting, population, or study design for the title. If the user has not specified the method, use a neutral phrase such as “a qualitative study.”

## Keywords

For Keywords, provide 4–8 terms that improve discoverability and accurately reflect the manuscript.

### Keyword selection

Keywords should usually cover:

1. Population
2. Core phenomenon
3. Method or design
4. Nursing field or care context
5. Theory, framework, or intervention if central to the study
6. Country, culture, or setting only when analytically important

### Recommended keyword types

Examples include:

- Qualitative research
- Nursing students
- Registered nurses
- Professional identity
- Clinical nursing
- Nursing education
- Older adults
- Self-management
- Patient experience
- Thematic analysis
- Content analysis
- Reflexive thematic analysis
- Life course
- Time geography
- Persona
- User profile
- End-of-life communication
- Nurse retention

### Avoid

Avoid overly broad keywords such as “health,” “care,” or “education” unless paired with a more specific term. Avoid keywords that do not appear in the manuscript or are not central to the study.

## Journal adaptation

For Journal adaptation, adjust the manuscript section according to the target journal’s scope, article type, style, and reporting expectations.

### Core journals

When the target journal is specified, adapt the writing to its likely expectations:

1. International Journal of Nursing Studies
   - Emphasise conceptual contribution, international relevance, methodological rigour, theory-informed interpretation, and implications for nursing knowledge and health systems.
   - Avoid overly local or descriptive framing unless the local context is used to develop transferable insights.

2. Journal of Advanced Nursing
   - Emphasise nursing practice, education, leadership, policy relevance, methodological transparency, and contribution to nursing science.
   - Make implications concrete and clearly linked to findings.

3. Nurse Education Today
   - Emphasise educational relevance, professional development, student learning, curriculum implications, clinical teaching, identity formation, and transition into practice.
   - Use a clear and accessible academic style.

4. Nurse Education in Practice
   - Emphasise practical educational implications, teaching strategies, learning experience, clinical placement, simulation, competence development, and student support.

5. BMC Nursing
   - Emphasise clarity, methodological transparency, practical relevance, and broad accessibility.
   - Avoid overly dense theoretical language unless essential.

6. Journal of Clinical Nursing
   - Emphasise clinical relevance, patient care, nursing roles, practice implications, and care quality.

7. Geriatric Nursing
   - Emphasise older adults, ageing contexts, long-term care, gerontological nursing practice, functional capacity, frailty, cognition, family care, and care environments.

### Adaptation rules

When adapting to a journal:
- Align the introduction with the journal’s disciplinary emphasis.
- Align the methods with reporting expectations such as COREQ or SRQR.
- Ensure results are sufficiently analytic, not merely descriptive.
- Ensure discussion highlights the journal-relevant contribution.
- Avoid making the manuscript sound like it belongs to a different discipline.

### Factuality rule

Do not claim that a journal requires a specific structure, word count, or reporting checklist unless the user provides the author guidelines or the requirement is verified. If uncertain, say that the section is adapted to the journal’s typical style rather than its formal requirements.

## COREQ/SRQR alignment

For qualitative nursing manuscripts, align reporting with COREQ or SRQR when appropriate.

### COREQ alignment

COREQ is particularly useful for interview and focus group studies. When checking or drafting a manuscript, consider whether the following information is reported:

1. Research team and reflexivity
   - Interviewer identity, credentials, occupation, experience, gender when relevant
   - Relationship with participants
   - Researcher assumptions, positionality, and reflexive strategies

2. Study design
   - Methodological orientation
   - Sampling strategy
   - Setting and recruitment
   - Participant characteristics
   - Interview or focus group procedures
   - Interview guide and pilot testing
   - Audio-recording, field notes, and transcription
   - Data saturation or dataset adequacy, when methodologically appropriate

3. Analysis and findings
   - Coding process
   - Number of coders or analytic roles, if relevant
   - Use of software, if applicable
   - Theme development
   - Participant quotations
   - Consistency between data and findings
   - Major and minor themes
   - Contrasting or deviant cases when relevant

### SRQR alignment

SRQR can be used for broader qualitative reporting. Consider whether the manuscript clearly reports:

1. Problem formulation and purpose
2. Qualitative approach and research paradigm
3. Researcher characteristics and reflexivity
4. Context and setting
5. Sampling strategy
6. Data collection methods
7. Data analysis process
8. Techniques to enhance trustworthiness
9. Findings and supporting evidence
10. Interpretation and implications
11. Limitations
12. Ethics and data confidentiality

### Alignment rules

Use reporting checklists to improve transparency, not as mechanical templates. Do not force unnecessary details into the manuscript if they are irrelevant to the method or journal.

For reflexive thematic analysis, avoid imposing inappropriate positivist criteria such as inter-coder reliability or consensus coding as mandatory. For qualitative content analysis, documenting coding discussions and category refinement may be appropriate.

### Missing information

If reporting details are missing, flag them clearly. Use a fill-in prompt such as:
- Please provide the interviewer’s background.
- Please provide the recruitment setting.
- Please provide the interview duration.
- Please provide the ethics approval number.
- Please clarify whether participant checking, peer debriefing, or audit trail was used.
- Please clarify whether saturation, information power, or dataset adequacy was used to justify sample size.

## Factuality and ethics rules

Maintain strict factuality when drafting, revising, or polishing qualitative nursing manuscripts.

### Do not fabricate

Do not invent:

1. Participant numbers
2. Demographic characteristics
3. Recruitment sites
4. Interview dates or duration
5. Inclusion or exclusion criteria
6. Sampling strategy
7. Interview guide content
8. Researcher characteristics
9. Reflexive practices
10. Analytic software
11. Coding process
12. Themes or subthemes
13. Participant quotations
14. Ethics approval numbers
15. Informed consent procedures
16. Data saturation or information power
17. Member checking or participant validation
18. Peer debriefing
19. References
20. Journal requirements

If information is missing, state what is missing and provide a placeholder or request-ready template.

### Participant quotations

Participant quotations must be preserved faithfully. Do not merge different participants’ words into one quotation. Do not embellish, over-polish, or alter the meaning of quotations.

When translating quotations, preserve the participant’s meaning, tone, uncertainty, and culturally specific expressions. If the manuscript requires it, note that quotations were translated from Chinese into English.

### Qualitative claims

Use cautious interpretive language. Prefer:
- Participants described...
- Participants’ accounts suggest...
- This theme reflects...
- These findings indicate...
- This may be understood as...
- This appears to reveal...

Avoid:
- This proves...
- This demonstrates causality...
- This significantly improves...
- This determines...
- This directly leads to...

### Literature and citations

Do not invent references. If the user asks for citations but has not provided literature, indicate where citations are needed or use placeholders such as “[citation needed]”. When online searching is available and requested, verify references before adding them.

### Ethics

Protect participant confidentiality. Do not include identifiable personal, institutional, or clinical details unless already anonymised and necessary. Use participant codes such as P1, N3, or Interviewee 5.

Do not generate or alter ethics approval numbers. If the ethics approval number is missing, use a placeholder.

## Output formats

When using this skill, select an output format based on the user’s task.

### 1. Full manuscript section

Use when the user asks to draft or rewrite a section.

Format:
- Section heading
- Polished academic text
- Optional notes on missing information or assumptions

### 2. Revision with explanation

Use when the user provides a draft and asks for improvement.

Format:
1. Revised version
2. Main changes made
3. Issues still requiring user confirmation

### 3. Diagnostic feedback

Use when the user asks whether a section is good or journal-appropriate.

Format:
1. Overall judgement
2. Strengths
3. Problems
4. Specific revision suggestions
5. Priority edits

### 4. Structured template

Use when the user is planning a new manuscript.

Format:
- Recommended section structure
- Writing prompts for each subsection
- Required information checklist
- Optional model sentences

### 5. Bilingual support

Use when the user provides Chinese material for an English manuscript.

Format:
1. Meaning-preserving English academic version
2. Optional Chinese explanation of logic or changes
3. Notes on terms that require user confirmation

### 6. COREQ/SRQR checklist review

Use when the user wants reporting-standard alignment.

Format:
1. Reported items
2. Missing or unclear items
3. Suggested additions
4. Text that can be inserted into the manuscript

### 7. Persona-based output

Use for persona or user-profile qualitative studies.

Format:
1. Persona name
2. Included participants
3. Core description
4. Key characteristics
5. Needs or support preferences
6. Representative quotations
7. Distinction from other personas
8. Implications for tailored intervention

### 8. Reviewer-response support

Use when the user asks to respond to reviewers.

Format:
1. Reviewer comment
2. Response strategy
3. Polite response text
4. Manuscript change
5. Location of revision, if provided by the user

### Missing information handling

When key information is missing, do not invent it. Provide a fill-in template, for example:

“Please provide: sample size, participant characteristics, recruitment setting, interview duration, analytic method, and ethics approval information.”

If the user needs an immediate draft, use bracketed placeholders such as:
- [sample size]
- [setting]
- [interview period]
- [analytic method]
- [ethics approval number]
- [participant quotation]
